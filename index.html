Section 4

MLA & Citation

Citation is not busywork — it is how you show your reader that your thinking is grounded in real sources. Learn the rules, then go deeper at Purdue OWL.

Rule 1 — The Basic Format
Place the author's last name and page number in parentheses at the end of the sentence, before the period. No comma between name and page number.
Golding depicts the conch as a symbol of order that the boys quickly abandon (Golding 16).
Common Error
(Golding, 16) | (Golding p. 16) | placing the citation after the period.
The parenthetical is part of the sentence — it lives inside the period, not after it.
Rule 2 — Signal Phrases (No Quote Bombs)
Never drop a quote into a sentence without a signal phrase that names the author or source. Integrate the quote grammatically.
As Golding suggests, the boys treat the conch as "a worthy plaything" rather than a tool of governance (16).
Common Error — Quote Bomb
"A worthy plaything" (Golding 16). The boys no longer respected the conch.
Every quote needs a host sentence. An unintroduced quote breaks the reader's trust and reads as careless.
Rule 3 — No Author Known / Website Sources
If no author is listed, use a shortened version of the title in quotation marks (for articles) or italics (for books or sites) in place of the author's name.
Studies suggest that close reading improves retention ("Reading Strategies" 4).
Common Error
Using the full title, using the URL, or skipping the citation entirely.
The shortened title points your reader to the correct Works Cited entry when no author exists.
Rule 4 — Block Quotations (Long Quotes)
If a quote runs longer than four lines of prose, format it as a block quotation: start on a new line, indent one inch, no quotation marks, citation after final punctuation.
The boys' descent begins almost immediately after their arrival:Ralph looked at the two boys with the conch and frowned.
The afternoon had advanced, and even the shadows on the
mountain had moved toward the sea. Ralph did not understand
what the darkness behind him was. (Golding 34)
Common Error
Putting quotation marks around a block quote, or using block format for short quotes.
Block formatting signals the passage is long enough to stand alone — the format itself argues for the quote's importance.

Book — Single Author
Format: Last, First. Title of Book. Publisher, Year.
Golding, William. Lord of the Flies. Faber and Faber, 1954.
Common Error
Golding, William. "Lord of the Flies." Faber and Faber, 1954. (Books are italicized, not in quotation marks.)
Italics signal a standalone work. Quotation marks signal a work within a larger work.
Article in a Journal or Magazine
Format: Last, First. "Article Title." Journal Name, vol. #, no. #, Year, pp. #–#.
Hynes, Samuel. "Several Interpretations of Lord of the Flies." English Journal, vol. 55, no. 2, 1966, pp. 40–45.
Common Error
Forgetting volume and issue numbers, or italicizing the article title instead of the journal name.
The article title gets quotes; the journal it lives in gets italics — same logic as book versus chapter.
Website / Webpage
Format: Last, First (if known). "Page Title." Website Name, Day Month Year, URL.
Brizee, Allen. "MLA Formatting and Style Guide." Purdue Online Writing Lab, 2 Mar. 2022, owl.purdue.edu/owl/research_and_citation/mla_style/mla_formatting_and_style_guide/mla_general_format.html.
Common Error
Listing only the homepage URL, omitting the author, or including "https://" (MLA omits the protocol).
A bare URL tells the reader nothing — the full entry gives them every tool to verify the source.
Chapter in an Edited Collection
Format: Last, First. "Chapter Title." Book Title, edited by First Last, Publisher, Year, pp. #–#.
Rosenfield, Claire. "Men of a Smaller Growth." Lord of the Flies: A Casebook, edited by James R. Baker, Prentice Hall, 1988, pp. 35–51.
Common Error
Treating the chapter like a standalone book, or omitting the editor's name.
The editor curated the collection — their name is part of the full record of the source.
Works Cited Page — Formatting Rules
These rules govern the entire page, not just individual entries.
  • "Works Cited" is centered at the top — not bold, not underlined, not in quotes
  • Entries are listed alphabetically by the author's last name (or title if no author)
  • The entire page is double-spaced, including the space between entries
  • Each entry uses a hanging indent: first line flush left, subsequent lines indented half an inch
  • The Works Cited page is its own page at the end — never tacked onto the last paragraph
